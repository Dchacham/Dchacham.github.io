Known Causes for Alzheimers

Alzheimers diease is a neurological disorder that is the most common cause of Dementia. Alzheimers is usually caused by combination of changes in the brain related to old age, genetics, lifestyle, and environmental factors. These factros can cause an abnormal build up of Tau and Amyloid proteins resulting in brain cells dying. The way this is caused is through amyloid proteins building up in the brain causing large masses (plaques) blocking the communications between nerve cells, preventing them from doing specific functions. In addition this also explains many of the symptoms experienced when having Alzheimers. Alzheimers is broken up into three main stages.The different stages of Alzheimers vary on the severity of ones condition. The three main stages of Alzheimers are mild, moderate, and late stage Alzheimers. There is also a stage before symptoms of Alzheimer's begin called preclinical Alzheimers. Death of nerve cells first begin in the hippocampus of the brain(controls meory) and then spreads to other areas.

Factors that increase risk of developing Alzheimers

A main factor that generally increases ones risk of developing Alzheimers is Heredity factors. Having a first degree relative (parent or sibling) will substantially increase once chance of developing Alzheimers. However, not everyone who has this gene will devlope Alzheimers and someone can also develope Alzheimers without having this gene. Lastly, Age is a sigificant risk factor. Alzheimers is usually Found in people above the age of 65. However, a small number of people devlope "early-onset" Alzheimers when they are between the ages of 30 and 40.

Mild Alzheimers (stage one)

Alzherimers first stage is knows as Early stage Alzheimers or mild. At this stage a person may still function independently. However, the person may begin to feel like they are having memory lapses, forgetting a word or where everyday possesions were placed. At this stage symtoms are not very noticable. Family and friends will most likely begin to notice and a doctor could identify the symptoms using certain diagnostic tools.



The symptoms at this stage include:

Coming up with the right word choice or name to express what you want to say
Remembering the names of new people
Difficulty carrying out certain tasks in social situations/ work settings
Forgetting material just read
Losing / misplacing valuable objects
Increased difficulty in organizing and planning ahead

Middle-stage/ Moderate stage Alzheimers.

Middle stage Alzheimers is commonly the longest stage of Alzheimers and can last many years. As the disease worsens the more care the person will require. During this stage the symtoms become fairly clear. In addition the symtoms that a person experiences ta this atge become more life altering and intense. The damage to nerve cells in the brain will progress making it difficult for the person to express there thoughts and carry out basic tasks without help. Symptoms can vary based on a person and there condition.

The most common symtoms of Middle stage Alzheimers include:

Forgetfulness of events and personal history/memories
Mood swings withdrawn from social, mental and difficult situations
Unable to recall personal information (contact info, address, schools attended)
Confusion about time or place
Change in sleep patterns from day to night
Person may experience bladder/ bowel issues
Personality, behavioral changes
Person may experience delusions compulsive and redundant behavior

Late stage Alzheimers

Late stage Alzheimers is the most severe stage. People suffering from Alzheimers will lose there ability to repond to there evironment. Carrying out a conversation will be exeedingly difficult for a person. In addition, a person may begin to move unctrollably. As a person memory continues to decline they will experience significant personality changes. Late stage Alzheimers requires intense and constant care. Although each perosn may experince symptoms differently.

The most common symtoms/ difficulties found at this stage include:

Losing awareness of recent situations/ experiences (surroundings)
Difficulty communicating
Becoming more at risk for infections especially pneumonia
Loses many physical abilities, including walking, sitting, eating.
May lose bowel and bladder control
May be able to say some words or phrases, but not have a conversation
Needs help with all activities all of the time
Is unaware of recent experiences and of his or her surroundings
